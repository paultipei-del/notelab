## The intuition

**Chord identification** is the skill of hearing a chord and recognizing its quality, is it major, minor, diminished, augmented, a dominant 7th, a minor 7th, a major 7th? More advanced practitioners can also identify the chord's inversion (root position, first inversion, second inversion).

This is harder than interval recognition because chords contain multiple pitches sounding simultaneously. Your ear has to separate the individual notes, or (more commonly with practice) hear the chord as a unified quality. Both approaches work; most experienced musicians do a mix.

Chord identification is essential for several musical activities:
- **Harmonic dictation**, writing down the chord progression of a piece from listening
- **Jazz analysis**, understanding complex jazz changes by ear
- **Transcription**, figuring out songs to play them yourself
- **Composition**, hearing what chord will fit where as you create music

## The basic triads, starting point

Before moving to seventh chords and inversions, master the four basic triad qualities:

**Major triad**, bright, stable, "happy." Example: C-E-G.

**Minor triad**, darker, more melancholy. Example: C-E♭-G.

**Diminished triad**, tense, unstable, sometimes described as "creepy." Example: C-E♭-G♭.

**Augmented triad**, unusual, mysterious, often described as "dreamy" or "unresolved." Example: C-E-G♯.

These four have distinct qualities that most ears can learn to distinguish within weeks of practice. Major and minor are the easiest (and most common in real music). Diminished and augmented are less common but have very distinctive sounds once you know them.

## Practice technique

**Listen repeatedly.** Play each of the four triads on a piano or use an ear training app. Play major, then minor, then diminished, then augmented. Hear them in sequence. Notice the differences.

**Practice in isolation.** Play a random chord quality (without telling yourself which). Try to identify it. Then check. Repeat many times.

**Practice in context.** Listen to music that uses these qualities. A major-key piece is heavy with major chords, with minor and diminished chords appearing at specific functions (ii, vi, vii°). A minor-key piece features minor chords prominently.

**Sing arpeggios.** Play a chord, then sing the three notes in arpeggiated form. This connects what you hear to what you produce, strengthening the identification skill.

## Moving to seventh chords

Once you can reliably identify triad qualities, move to seventh chords:

**Major 7 (Cmaj7)**, major triad + major 7th above root. Bright, sweet, jazzy. Sounds like the first chord of "Nature Boy" or "Have You Met Miss Jones?"

**Dominant 7 (C7)**, major triad + minor 7th. Tense, wanting to resolve, bluesy. The core chord of the blues and of almost every V-I cadence.

**Minor 7 (Cm7)**, minor triad + minor 7th. Dark, smooth, jazzy. Sounds like many jazz and pop chords.

**Half-diminished 7 (Cø7 or Cm7♭5)**, diminished triad + minor 7th. Tense but smooth, common in minor-key music.

**Fully diminished 7 (C°7)**, diminished triad + diminished 7th. Highly tense, dramatic. Often used in late Romantic and film music for moments of suspense.

Seventh chords have four pitches each, making them more complex to identify than triads. But most people can develop reliable seventh-chord identification within a few months of regular practice, starting from strong triad identification skills.

## Practice tips for seventh chords

**Focus on the added 7th.** Once you know the basic triad (major, minor, or diminished), listen for the quality of the added 7th note. A minor 7th sounds different from a major 7th. The triad plus the 7th's quality together define the chord.

**Compare adjacent qualities.** Play a dominant 7 (C-E-G-B♭) and compare to a major 7 (C-E-G-B). Both have major triads; they differ only in the 7th. Hearing them side-by-side trains your ear to focus on the 7th's color.

**Use rewarding contexts.** Blues music is rich with dominant 7 chords. Jazz ballads use many major 7 and minor 7 chords. Diminished 7 chords appear in dramatic classical and film contexts. Listen to music that uses each quality and notice how it sounds.

## Chord inversions

More advanced: identifying chord inversions by ear.

**Root position**, the root is in the bass. The chord sounds most stable and "grounded."

**First inversion**, the third is in the bass. The chord sounds slightly off-balance but still clear.

**Second inversion**, the fifth is in the bass. The chord sounds very unstable, "waiting" to move somewhere.

Inversion recognition requires hearing which chord tone sits at the bottom. This takes additional practice beyond basic chord identification. Most musicians develop inversion recognition after they've solidified quality identification.

**Test:** Play a C major triad in each inversion. Root position (C-E-G) has a solid, grounded feel. First inversion (E-G-C) has a slightly "floating" quality. Second inversion (G-C-E) has the most unusual, unstable feel. Listen repeatedly; the patterns become recognizable.

## Common questions

**Can I identify chords without identifying their inversion?**

Yes, and most basic chord identification focuses on quality alone (is it major, minor, etc.) without worrying about inversion. Inversion recognition is a separate skill that builds on top of quality recognition.

**How do I handle chords with added extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths)?**

Learn the basic triads and sevenths first. Once those are solid, listen for the "extra color" added by extensions. A 9th chord sounds like a 7th chord with "one more brightness" added on top. An 11th or 13th adds increasing complexity and sophistication. Developing an ear for these extensions takes considerable time; they're mostly relevant in jazz.

**What about very complex jazz chords like altered dominants (V7♯9 or V7♯11)?**

These are the domain of advanced jazz ear training. Most musicians learn the basic qualities and basic sevenths first, then spend years developing ears for the more exotic flavors. Jazz musicians with strong ears often recognize these through their characteristic "tension", the specific clashes they produce against the tonic.

**How do I identify chord progressions?**

Chord identification is the first step; recognizing how chords connect (progressions) is a related but separate skill. Typical training: listen to a progression, identify the Roman numeral of each chord, hear the functional relationships (I → IV → V → I, for example). Harmonic dictation (the skill of writing down heard chord progressions) is the full form of this practice. It takes time to develop but is eminently learnable.
