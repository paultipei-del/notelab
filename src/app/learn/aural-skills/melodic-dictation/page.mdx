## The intuition

**Melodic dictation** is the practice of listening to a melody (typically played multiple times) and writing it down in musical notation. You hear, you remember, you transcribe. Every pitch and every rhythm must be correctly identified and written.

This is one of the most demanding aural skills because it integrates several sub-skills at once:
- **Pitch identification**, recognizing each pitch's position in the key
- **Interval recognition**, hearing the distance between consecutive notes
- **Rhythm perception**, hearing the meter, note values, and rhythmic patterns
- **Memory**, holding the melody in your head long enough to write it
- **Key identification**, knowing what key the melody is in

Melodic dictation is a centerpiece of traditional ear training curricula. Conservatories and music schools assign dictation exercises regularly. With practice, even long and complex melodies become transcribable.

## A strategy for melodic dictation

**Step 1: Identify the key.** Before the melody plays (or after the first playing), determine the key. This gives you a framework for hearing pitches as scale degrees.

**Step 2: Identify the meter and tempo.** Count the first few beats to establish the pulse and determine the time signature. Is it in 4/4? 3/4? 6/8?

**Step 3: Listen to the overall shape.** On the first playing, don't try to catch every note. Get a general sense of the melody's contour, where it rises, where it falls, where it climaxes.

**Step 4: Write the rhythm first.** Many skilled transcribers start by writing just the rhythm (with unfixed pitches) on subsequent playings. Get the note values correct before worrying about specific pitches.

**Step 5: Fill in the pitches.** With the rhythm established, add specific pitches. Work from landmarks (the first note, the last note, the highest note) and interpolate between them using interval recognition.

**Step 6: Check and refine.** Sing back what you've written. Does it match what you heard? If not, adjust. Multiple playings let you catch and correct errors.

The exact order of these steps varies with the specific exercise and with personal preference. Some people prefer to capture a rough contour first; others prefer to nail the rhythm and then add pitches. Both approaches work.

## Common pitfalls

**Trying to catch too much at once.** Beginning dictators often want to write down every note on the first playing. This is usually impossible and leads to errors. Be patient, listen first, write later, use multiple playings.

**Losing the key.** In longer melodies, the tonal center can drift in perception if the melody modulates or explores far-flung pitches. Keep the tonic firmly in mind as your reference.

**Rushing the rhythm.** Writing the rhythm last is usually a mistake. Lock in the rhythm early, it's often easier to hear than specific pitches, and it provides a framework for the pitches.

**Ignoring landmarks.** A melody's highest note, lowest note, opening note, and ending note are landmarks you should catch immediately. These anchor the rest of the transcription.

**Not singing back.** Singing what you've written as you go confirms what you've heard. If you can sing the melody from your transcription and it matches what was played, you're probably correct.

## Difficulty levels

**Beginning**, short melodies (2-4 measures) with simple rhythms (whole notes, half notes, quarter notes) in familiar keys.

**Intermediate**, longer melodies (8-16 measures) with more complex rhythms (eighth notes, dotted rhythms) in various keys including some with several sharps or flats.

**Advanced**, longer melodies (16-32 measures or longer) with complex rhythms, chromatic pitches, wide intervallic leaps, and potentially modulations. Possibly in less common meters (6/8, 5/4).

Progress through these levels gradually. Don't try to jump from beginning exercises directly to advanced ones; the gap is too large. Work through intermediate exercises until they feel comfortable before moving up.

## Using solfège during dictation

If you know solfège, it's a powerful tool for dictation:

- Hear the melody's contour
- Mentally convert each note to its solfège syllable
- Write the pitches corresponding to those syllables

For example, if the melody in C major goes do-re-mi-sol-fa-mi-do, you've effectively identified it in solfège. Writing it as pitches (C-D-E-G-F-E-C) becomes straightforward.

Solfège acts as a bridge between hearing and notation. You don't have to convert raw pitches to notation; you convert through the syllable intermediary.

## Practice strategies

**Use recordings or an instructor.** Many online resources and textbooks provide dictation exercises. Apps like EarMaster and Teoria include dictation practice with feedback.

**Start slow.** Work on very short melodies first (2 measures, 4 beats) before attempting longer ones. Even short dictations build valuable skills.

**Self-dictate.** Write a melody yourself (without looking at it), have a friend or computer play it back, and transcribe it. You know what you meant to write, which lets you check accuracy easily.

**Transcribe recorded music.** Pick simple pop songs or folk tunes and try to notate their melodies. This combines skill development with the fun of getting to know familiar music more deeply.

**Combine with other skills.** Dictation integrates many skills. If you're weak in rhythm, dictation reveals it. If your interval recognition is off, dictation shows you. Use dictation as a diagnostic tool to identify weaknesses and target specific practice.

## Common questions

**How many times should a melody be played in dictation?**

Traditionally 3-4 times for shorter exercises; up to 6-8 times for longer, more complex ones. Each playing provides more information, the first gives you the overall shape, the second lets you lock in some details, and so on. Don't try to extract everything from one listening.

**What if the melody modulates?**

Modulation makes dictation harder. Keep your ear on the tonal center at each moment. When the key changes, adjust your reference. Exercises involving modulation are usually advanced; basic dictation works in a single key.

**Do I have to write everything perfectly, or is an approximation acceptable?**

For learning purposes, approximations are fine early on. The goal is to get closer and closer to accuracy. For formal exams, accuracy is expected, wrong notes or rhythms lose points. Don't stress about perfection in practice; focus on gradual improvement.

**How does this relate to sight-singing?**

Sight-singing and dictation are complementary. Sight-singing goes from notation to sound (you read music and sing it). Dictation goes from sound to notation (you hear music and write it). Both involve understanding the same relationships (pitches, intervals, rhythms) from different directions. Strong sight-singers usually become strong dictators, and vice versa.
