## The intuition

**Sight-singing** is the skill of reading a melody from a score and singing it correctly, without hearing it first. You look at the notation, and you produce the sounds directly, no instrument, no recording, just your voice and your musical imagination.

This is one of the most comprehensive aural skills because it requires you to integrate nearly everything:
- Interval recognition (you need to sing the correct intervals)
- Pitch imagination (you need to hear each note in your head before singing)
- Rhythm reading (you need to perform the correct rhythms)
- Key and scale context (you need to know the tonal framework)
- Physical vocal skill (you need to produce the pitches accurately)

Sight-singing is a hallmark of serious music education. Conservatories and music schools test it regularly. It's demanding, but it's also profoundly useful, it directly proves that you understand musical notation at a deep level.

## The approach

**1. Scan the score first.** Before you start singing:
- **Identify the clef.** Treble? Bass? Alto?
- **Identify the key.** Look at the key signature.
- **Identify the time signature.** Is it 4/4, 3/4, 6/8?
- **Note the tempo.** Is it fast, moderate, slow?
- **Scan the range.** How high and how low does the melody go?
- **Look for challenging intervals or rhythms.** Are there any tricky leaps or unusual rhythms to prepare for?

This scanning takes just a few seconds but orients you to the piece.

**2. Establish the tonic.** Before singing, imagine the tonic pitch (the "do" of the key). You can use a reference pitch (a tuning fork, a keyboard, or even your own pitch memory) to find it. Hum it internally to establish the key.

**3. Sing the first note.** The first note might not be the tonic, but you can figure out what interval it is from the tonic and sing accordingly. If the first note is scale degree 3 (mi), sing up from do to mi before starting.

**4. Sing through the melody.** Move one note at a time, using whatever technique helps you, solfège syllables, scale degree numbers, or just the pitches.

**5. Listen to yourself.** Pay attention to whether you're singing accurately. If you get off-key, you'll hear it. Adjust and return to the correct pitches.

## Using solfège

For sight-singing, solfège is often the most effective tool. Instead of singing the pitch letters or numbers, sing the syllables: do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do.

**Why solfège works:**

- **Each syllable has a consistent vowel**, making vocal production easy
- **The syllables represent scale degrees**, so you hear each note's function in the key
- **The same syllable in different keys** produces the same tonal relationship (do in G major, do in E♭ major, both function as the tonic)

Singing "do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do" should come to feel like singing "1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8" in terms of the pitch relationships. With practice, the syllables become automatic, you see a note, instantly know its syllable, and sing that pitch.

## Progressive difficulty

**Beginner**, simple melodies in C major or G major, using only stepwise motion (no leaps larger than a third), rhythms with whole, half, and quarter notes, and a small range (an octave or less).

**Intermediate**, melodies in various keys, with some leaps (4ths and 5ths), more rhythmic variety (dotted rhythms, eighth notes), and a larger range.

**Advanced**, complex melodies with wide leaps (6ths, 7ths, octaves), chromatic alterations, complex rhythms including tuplets, modulations, and extended range.

**Expert**, long, intricate melodies in any key, with advanced harmonic chromaticism, unusual meters (7/8, 5/4), and rapid rhythmic changes.

Progress through these levels gradually. Don't jump from beginner to intermediate too fast; build fluency at each level before moving up.

## Common challenges

**Wide leaps.** Leaping a 6th, 7th, or octave is harder than moving stepwise. Practice these specific leaps in isolation. Pre-hear the target note before singing it.

**Chromatic alterations.** Raised or lowered syllables (di, ri, me, le, etc.) take extra practice to sing in tune. Practice chromatic scales and altered chord tones.

**Rhythmic accuracy.** Focus on the rhythm as carefully as the pitch. Clap the rhythm first if needed. Use a metronome to stay in time.

**Staying in key.** Long melodies can cause the tonic to drift in perception. Keep the tonic firmly in mind. Periodically re-imagine do to check your tuning.

**Range challenges.** Melodies that go outside your comfortable vocal range are hard to sing. If the piece is too high or low, transpose it mentally to a more comfortable range (the interval relationships stay the same).

## Practice strategies

**Start small.** Work on short exercises (2-4 measures) before attempting longer ones. Master each short exercise before moving on.

**Sing regularly.** Daily sight-singing practice, even 10 minutes, produces rapid improvement. Regularity beats intensity.

**Use progressive exercises.** Sight-singing textbooks (like *A New Approach to Sight Singing* by Berkowitz, Fontrier, and Kraft, or other standard texts) provide exercises organized by difficulty. Work through them systematically.

**Sing unfamiliar music.** The point of sight-singing is to sing music you haven't seen before. Always work on new material, not just review.

**Sing in ensembles.** Choral singing, duet singing, or any other ensemble singing reinforces sight-singing skills. You get immediate feedback on your accuracy, and you have to stay in time with others.

**Record yourself.** Sometimes what you think you're singing is different from what you're actually producing. Recording reveals errors that you don't hear in the moment.

## Common questions

**Do I need a good voice to sight-sing?**

Not necessarily. Sight-singing is about pitch and rhythm accuracy, not beauty of tone. Many excellent sight-singers don't have particularly beautiful voices, they're just very accurate. If you can sing in tune and in time, you can sight-sing well regardless of your vocal quality.

**What if I can't match pitches?**

This is a separate skill (**vocal pitch matching**) and if it's a problem, you need to work on it specifically. Most people can match pitches with practice, but some people need specific exercises. Sing along with simple melodies, using a piano or recording for reference. If pitch matching is genuinely difficult, a vocal teacher can diagnose and address the issue.

**Should I use solfège or numbers or pitch letters?**

Solfège is usually the most effective for sight-singing, for the reasons mentioned. Numbers work too. Pitch letters (calling out C, D, E, etc.) are less commonly used for sight-singing because they don't convey tonal function as clearly. Whichever system you use, consistency helps, don't mix systems in a single exercise.

**How does sight-singing relate to sight-reading on an instrument?**

They're related but different. Sight-reading on piano involves finger placement and eye-hand coordination; sight-singing involves vocal production and pitch imagination. Both require fluent reading of the notation. Many musicians develop one skill more than the other, but strong sight-singers often become strong sight-readers and vice versa because both depend on fluent musical imagination.
