## The intuition

**Solfège** is the system of singing scale degrees using specific syllables: do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do. Each syllable corresponds to a scale degree, do is the tonic (1), re is the supertonic (2), mi is the mediant (3), and so on. Singing a melody using solfège syllables reveals its tonal structure. You don't just sing "a note"; you sing "the third scale degree" or "the leading tone." The syllables make tonal function audible.

Solfège has been taught for centuries and remains one of the most powerful tools for developing musical hearing. Once you've internalized the syllables, you hear music differently, you hear where each note sits in its key, what role it plays in the harmony, how it relates to the tonic. It's like learning the names of a city's neighborhoods so you can navigate instead of just wandering.

This page covers the basic solfège system, the distinction between fixed-do and movable-do, and how to practice solfège as a foundation for other aural skills.

## The seven basic syllables

The core solfège syllables for a major scale:

- **Do**, 1st degree (tonic)
- **Re**, 2nd degree (supertonic)
- **Mi**, 3rd degree (mediant)
- **Fa**, 4th degree (subdominant)
- **Sol**, 5th degree (dominant)
- **La**, 6th degree (submediant)
- **Ti**, 7th degree (leading tone)
- **Do**, 8th degree (tonic, one octave up)

In C major, do is C, re is D, mi is E, and so on up to do (high C). In G major, do is G, re is A, mi is B, and so on. The syllables stay tied to scale degrees, regardless of key.

This is the "movable-do" system, do moves to whichever note is the tonic. It's the most common system used in American, British, and many other English-language music programs.

## Fixed-do vs. movable-do

Two systems exist:

**Movable-do**, the syllables move with the key. In C major, do = C. In G major, do = G. In any major key, do is always the tonic.

**Fixed-do**, the syllables are tied to specific pitches. Do always equals C, re always equals D, mi always equals E, regardless of what key you're in. In G major, the tonic (G) is called "sol" because G is sol, not do.

Both systems have strengths:

**Movable-do strengths:**
- Makes tonal function audible, do is always the tonic, so you hear it as home regardless of key
- Ear training is consistent across keys, the interval from do to mi is always a major 3rd
- Sight-singing is easier because the syllables always represent scale degrees

**Fixed-do strengths:**
- Matches absolute pitch development (do is always the same pitch)
- Consistent across atonal music (no "tonic" to relate to)
- Standard in European conservatories, especially French and Italian traditions

Movable-do dominates American music education and is generally recommended for ear training. Fixed-do dominates in conservatory training in many European countries. Both produce excellent musicians when taught well.

This page focuses on movable-do, since that's the most common starting point in American contexts.

## Chromatic syllables

What about notes outside the basic major scale, sharps and flats? Movable-do uses altered syllables:

**Raised syllables (sharps)**, change the vowel to "i":
- Di (raised do), between do and re
- Ri (raised re), between re and mi
- Fi (raised fa), between fa and sol
- Si (raised sol), between sol and la
- Li (raised la), between la and ti

**Lowered syllables (flats)**, change the vowel to "a" (usually "e"):
- Ra (lowered re), between do and re
- Me (lowered mi), between re and mi
- Le (lowered la), between sol and la
- Te (lowered ti), between la and ti
- Fe (lowered fa), rare; between mi and fa

For a chromatic scale starting from C, the syllables ascending are:
do, di, re, ri, mi, fa, fi, sol, si, la, li, ti, do

And descending:
do, ti, te, la, le, sol, se, fa, mi, me, re, ra, do

(Different traditions use slightly different chromatic syllables. Te and le for the descending flats are standard in American practice.)

These chromatic syllables let you sing any pitch in the 12-note system, not just the seven diatonic notes.

## Minor key solfège

For minor keys, two approaches exist:

**La-based minor**, the minor tonic is "la." In A minor, A = la, B = ti, C = do, D = re, and so on. This reflects the fact that natural minor is the Aeolian mode starting on the 6th degree of the major scale. If you know C major's solfège (where A = la), you're also "in" A natural minor when you think of A as the tonic, you just have to remember that your tonic is la, not do.

**Do-based minor**, the minor tonic is "do." Flats are added to reflect the minor scale structure: do, re, me, fa, sol, le, te, do (for natural minor). This makes the syllable system treat do as the tonic regardless of whether you're in major or minor.

Both approaches have advocates. La-based minor is traditional in classical music education and reflects the natural relationship between major and its relative minor. Do-based minor treats major and minor as "parallel" systems and simplifies the concept of tonic = do across all keys.

Many teachers use la-based minor for early training and then introduce do-based minor as students mature. The specific choice depends on your teacher and tradition.

## Hand signs

A powerful extension of solfège is the **Curwen hand sign system**, where each syllable has an associated hand position:

- Do, fist at waist
- Re, flat hand at diagonal (like shaking hands)
- Mi, flat hand horizontal (palm down)
- Fa, thumb pointing down
- Sol, flat hand vertical (like a salute)
- La, hand in C-shape
- Ti, finger pointing up
- Do, fist above head

Hand signs add a physical dimension to solfège. Singing while showing the signs reinforces memory and helps internalize the syllable-pitch connection. Hand signs are especially common in Kodály-based music education (particularly for children) but work well at any level.

## Why solfège matters for ear training

Solfège converts abstract pitch relationships into a concrete syllable system. Each syllable has its own "feel":
- **Do** feels stable (home)
- **Mi** feels warm and confident (major 3rd, the characteristic major sound)
- **Fa** feels slightly restless (wants to move somewhere)
- **Sol** feels strong and resolved (the dominant)
- **Ti** feels urgent (leading tone wanting to resolve to do)

When you sing solfège, you're not just producing pitches, you're producing scale-degree functions. This makes tonal music understandable at a deep level. You hear a pitch in context, not just as an isolated note.

As you develop, you can identify a melody's solfège just by listening. This means you've internalized the tonal structure: you're hearing the music's grammar, not just its surface.

## Common questions

**Why are the syllables "do, re, mi" specifically?**

They come from a Latin hymn used by Guido of Arezzo (c. 991-1033), where each line started on a successively higher pitch. The first syllables of each line (ut, re, mi, fa, sol, la) became the solfège syllables. "Ut" was later changed to "do" (easier to sing), and "si" (later changed to "ti") was added for the 7th degree. The system evolved over centuries but keeps its medieval roots.

**Can I do ear training without solfège?**

Yes, though it's harder. Some approaches use letter names (C, D, E) or numbers (1, 2, 3) instead. Solfège has historically been preferred because its syllables are designed to be sung easily and its fixed vowels support consistent vocal production. But other systems work for those who prefer them.

**Do I have to use movable-do if I'm a classical musician?**

Not necessarily. Classical musicians in European traditions often use fixed-do. In the United States and much of Asia, movable-do is more common. Both are legitimate. Movable-do is generally considered better for ear training and tonal hearing; fixed-do is better for absolute pitch training and atonal contexts.

**How do I learn solfège quickly?**

Start simple. Learn the basic major scale syllables (do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do). Sing familiar melodies in solfège, folk songs like "Mary Had a Little Lamb" or "Oh When the Saints." Practice consistently, a few minutes a day. Within a month of regular practice, you'll have the basic syllables memorized and usable.
