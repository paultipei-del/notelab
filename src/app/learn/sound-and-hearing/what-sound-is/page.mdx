## The intuition

When something vibrates (a guitar string, a drumhead, the vocal cords in your throat) it pushes against the air around it. Those pushes ripple outward as waves of slightly denser and slightly thinner air, traveling until they reach your ears. Your eardrum, a thin membrane, moves in response to those pressure changes, and your brain translates the movement into what we call sound.

That's all sound is, at its root: a pattern of air pressure changing over time. Everything we'll talk about in music (pitch, harmony, rhythm, timbre) comes from this simple physical fact.

## What makes one sound different from another

Not all vibrations sound the same. Two qualities of a vibration determine most of what we hear:

**How fast it vibrates** (frequency) determines how *high* or *low* the sound is. A slow vibration (say, 100 cycles per second) sounds low. A fast vibration (1,000 cycles per second) sounds high. Frequency is measured in Hertz (Hz), which simply means "cycles per second." Human hearing ranges roughly from 20 Hz at the bottom to 20,000 Hz at the top, though the upper limit shrinks with age.

**How strong the vibration is** (amplitude) determines how *loud* it sounds. A small vibration moves the air gently and sounds soft; a large vibration moves the air forcefully and sounds loud. Amplitude is usually measured in decibels (dB), a logarithmic scale where each increase of about 10 dB is perceived as roughly twice as loud.

A third quality, **timbre**, is what lets you tell a violin from a flute playing the same pitch at the same volume. Timbre comes from the fact that real-world sounds aren't pure single vibrations, they're complex mixtures of many vibrations happening at once. We'll unpack this on the next page.

## Sound needs a medium

Sound can't travel through empty space. It needs something to push against, air, usually, but also water, wood, metal, or anything else that can compress and expand. This is why an astronaut floating in the vacuum of space hears nothing, even if something dramatic happens a few meters away.

Different mediums carry sound at different speeds. In air at room temperature, sound travels roughly 343 meters per second, which is why you see lightning before you hear thunder. In water, sound travels about four times faster. In solid steel, it travels about fifteen times faster than in air.

## Why this matters for music

Every instrument you'll ever play or hear is a device for producing specific vibrations. A violin does it by drawing a bow across a string. A flute does it by directing an air column across an opening. A piano does it by striking a tuned wire with a felt hammer. The physical mechanics differ wildly, but the result is the same: a pattern of air pressure changes, traveling outward, reaching ears.

Music theory (the subject of this entire reference) is really the study of how *organized* patterns of these vibrations produce the experiences we call melody, harmony, and rhythm. Understanding sound as vibration isn't a tangential starting point. It's the bedrock the rest of the building rests on.

## Common questions

**What's the difference between sound and noise?**

Physically, none. Both are vibrations in the air. Musically, we tend to call a sound "noise" when its vibrations are irregular, a chaotic mix of frequencies with no clear pattern. A sustained musical tone, by contrast, has a regular repeating pattern, which is what gives it a sense of pitch.

**Why do some sounds feel harsh and others feel smooth?**

The shape of the pressure wave matters. A smooth, rounded wave (like a pure tone from a tuning fork) sounds clean and simple. A jagged wave (one with sharp, abrupt changes) has many frequencies packed into it and tends to sound harsh or buzzy. Most musical instruments produce waves that sit somewhere in between.

**Can sound push things?**

Yes, surprisingly. Loud enough sound can move physical objects. Opera singers famously break wine glasses by matching the glass's natural resonant frequency with enough volume to shake it apart. In everyday music, the pressure changes are far too small to notice, but they are, physically, real pushes against everything they touch.
