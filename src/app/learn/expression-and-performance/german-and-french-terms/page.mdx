## The intuition

While Italian dominates music's international vocabulary, some composers chose to write performance markings in their own languages. German composers (Schumann, Brahms, Mahler, Wagner) often used German expressions. French composers (Debussy, Ravel, Satie) often used French. These markings are less standardized than Italian terms but appear regularly in Romantic and 20th-century scores.

Learning some basic German and French music terminology opens up large areas of repertoire. You don't need to be fluent in either language, just to recognize the most common terms when they appear.

## German performance terms

Common German markings:

**Tempo and motion:**
- **Langsam**, slow
- **Mäßig**, moderate
- **Bewegt**, moving, animated
- **Lebhaft**, lively
- **Schnell**, fast
- **Sehr**, very (*sehr langsam* = very slow)
- **Nicht zu**, not too (*nicht zu schnell* = not too fast)
- **Ruhig**, peaceful, calm

**Character:**
- **Mit Ausdruck**, with expression
- **Mit Empfindung**, with feeling
- **Innig**, heartfelt, intimate
- **Zart**, tender, delicate
- **Kräftig**, strong, powerful
- **Feierlich**, solemn
- **Sehnsuchtsvoll**, longingly, yearning
- **Heiter**, cheerful, bright

**Structural and dynamic:**
- **Leise**, quietly
- **Laut**, loudly
- **Allmählich**, gradually
- **Plötzlich**, suddenly
- **Immer**, always
- **Werden**, becoming (*leiser werden* = becoming quieter)

Schumann was particularly fond of German character markings. His piano pieces often carry evocative titles and character descriptions in German (*Träumerei*, "dreaming"; *Aufschwung*, "soaring"; *Warum?*, "why?"). Mahler's symphonies use German markings extensively for tempo, dynamics, and character, his instructions are often very specific ("*tempo primo*, but with more flow").

Wagner wrote opera scores with detailed German instructions, shaping every phrase's character. Reading his scores in the original German is essential for understanding his intentions.

## French performance terms

Common French markings:

**Tempo and motion:**
- **Lent**, slow
- **Modéré**, moderate
- **Vif**, lively
- **Animé**, animated
- **Très**, very
- **Peu à peu**, little by little
- **Un peu**, a little

**Character:**
- **Doucement**, gently, softly
- **Avec douceur**, with sweetness/gentleness
- **Avec grâce**, with grace
- **Avec élégance**, with elegance
- **Avec expression**, with expression
- **Avec mouvement**, with motion
- **Avec émotion**, with emotion
- **Calme**, calm
- **Tendrement**, tenderly

**Structural:**
- **Fin**, end
- **Coda** (used in French as in other languages)
- **Reprendre**, to take up again (similar to *a tempo*)
- **Mesuré**, measured, in strict time

Debussy favored French for his character markings, often writing poetic and evocative instructions. *Clair de lune*'s "Avec une expression profonde" ("with deep expression"), *La fille aux cheveux de lin*'s "Très calme et doucement expressif" ("very calm and sweetly expressive"), these markings give the music a specific emotional character.

Ravel, Fauré, and many other French composers similarly used French performance terms. Erik Satie took a distinctive approach, his markings often include surreal or humorous instructions in French ("Muffled, yellow," "Like a nightingale with a toothache"), giving his music a unique atmosphere.

## When you might encounter each

**German markings** appear in:
- Much German Romantic repertoire (Schumann, Brahms, Wagner, Mahler)
- German Romantic Lieder (art songs by Schubert, Schumann, Brahms, Wolf)
- Early 20th-century Austro-German music (Strauss, Zemlinsky, Schoenberg)
- Some operatic and oratorio traditions

**French markings** appear in:
- French Romantic and Impressionist music (Debussy, Ravel, Fauré)
- Early 20th-century French music (Satie, Poulenc, Milhaud, Boulez)
- Many French operas and ballets
- Some 19th-century Paris-based composers of other nationalities

You'll also encounter Italian markings throughout this repertoire, composers often mixed languages, using Italian for standard terms and their native language for more specific expressive content.

## Handling unfamiliar terms

When you see an unfamiliar performance marking:

**Check the language.** German markings often begin with capital letters (nouns in German are capitalized). French has distinctive accents (é, è, ê). Italian words typically end in vowels.

**Use a music dictionary.** Most comprehensive music dictionaries include German and French terms alongside Italian. Online resources like the *New Grove Dictionary* cover them.

**Consider context.** Even without knowing the exact translation, you can often infer meaning from context, a marking at the beginning of a piece is likely a tempo or character indication; a marking with a hairpin is a dynamic change.

**Ask or research.** If you're preparing a piece seriously, look up unfamiliar markings. The composer included them for a reason, and understanding them shapes your interpretation.

## Common questions

**Why didn't composers just use Italian?**

Partly nationalism. Romantic-era composers were often deeply invested in their national identity, and using their native languages for performance markings expressed that. Schumann wrote in German because he was a German composer writing for German musicians. Partly also precision, native-language markings can express subtleties that translation might lose. Schumann's *innig* doesn't translate easily to Italian; it's a specific German concept of inward, intimate feeling.

**Do I have to learn German and French to play Romantic music?**

Not learn them fully, just recognize the most common performance terms. You can play Schumann without knowing German, but you play him better if you understand his German markings. Similarly for Debussy and French. Most performers eventually build a working vocabulary of 20-30 terms in each language.

**What about composers from other nationalities, Russian, Czech, English?**

Less standardized, but they exist. Russian composers (Tchaikovsky, Rachmaninoff) usually used Italian but sometimes added Russian instructions. Czech composers (Dvořák, Janáček) used Czech terms especially for character. English composers (Elgar, Vaughan Williams, Britten) often used English for character markings. When you encounter unfamiliar markings, the language usually gives you a clue about the composer's nationality or tradition.

**Are these markings always translated in modern editions?**

Often, yes. Modern critical editions frequently include translations of non-Italian markings in footnotes or brackets. But the original markings remain, they're historical and reflect the composer's intent. A modern edition of Schumann will usually keep his German markings while adding English translations for clarity.
