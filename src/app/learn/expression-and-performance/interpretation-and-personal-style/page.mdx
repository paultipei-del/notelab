## The intuition

A score is not a complete specification of a performance. It's a skeleton, a collection of instructions that guides the performer, but doesn't prescribe every detail. Two pianists playing the same Beethoven sonata from the same edition will produce noticeably different performances, even though they're reading the same markings. This isn't a flaw in notation; it's a feature. Music notation leaves space for **interpretation**, the performer's act of bringing the score to life.

This page is about that space. What do performers actually decide? How do they develop a personal style? How does interpretation relate to the composer's markings? And what makes one performance "better" than another, given that there's no single correct version of any piece?

## What the score leaves open

Even a well-marked score leaves many things to the performer:

**Exact tempo.** A tempo marking like "Allegro" spans roughly 120-156 BPM. The specific choice within that range is interpretive.

**Tempo flexibility.** Within a movement, how much to push, pull, or breathe? The score rarely specifies every micro-fluctuation.

**Dynamic shading.** Between marked dynamics, how does the volume shape? Does a "forte" passage have gradual crescendos and decrescendos internally, or is it uniform?

**Phrasing decisions.** Even with marked slurs, where does each phrase peak? Which notes carry more weight? Where does the performer breathe?

**Articulation nuance.** A staccato note is short, but how short? How much space between it and the next note?

**Balance.** In multi-voice music, how prominent is each voice? Which line leads at any given moment?

**Timbre.** On instruments with tonal variety (piano, bowed strings, voice), what color does each phrase have?

**Rubato.** The subtle timing adjustments that make music feel alive rather than metronomic.

**Character.** The overall feel, aggressive or gentle, grand or intimate, serious or playful?

Each of these is a decision the performer makes, implicitly or explicitly. Different performers make different decisions, producing different performances.

## The spectrum from faithful to free

Interpretations exist on a spectrum:

**Faithful interpretation.** Follows every marking as closely as possible. Metronome markings exactly. Dynamics precisely at the indicated levels. Articulations strictly as marked. Works well for clarity and honors the composer's explicit intentions.

**Informed interpretation.** Follows markings but applies stylistic knowledge. A Baroque piece played with appropriate ornamentation (beyond what's explicitly notated). A Romantic piece with rubato that matches the era's expectations. Historical performance practice informs the approach.

**Personal interpretation.** The performer makes significant individual choices beyond just the markings. They might take a slower or faster tempo than suggested, add their own dynamic shaping, emphasize different voices, or shape phrases in ways the composer didn't specify.

**Free interpretation.** The performer treats the score as raw material for extensive creative reworking. This might include significant deviations from markings, added ornamentation, rhythmic adjustments, or other changes. Common in jazz standards, folk music, and some avant-garde reinterpretations of classical works.

Most performers work somewhere between "informed" and "personal" interpretations. Pure faithful interpretations can feel dry; overly free interpretations can feel disrespectful to the composer. The balance is a matter of style, repertoire, and personal taste.

## Developing personal style

How does a performer develop their own voice? A few recognized pathways:

**Listening to many performances.** Hearing different interpretations of the same piece trains your ear to the range of possibilities. You start noticing what works, what doesn't, and what speaks to you.

**Studying with teachers.** A good teacher can introduce you to interpretive traditions and help you find your own approach within them. Different teachers have different perspectives; many performers study with several over time.

**Analyzing scores deeply.** Understanding a piece's structure, harmony, and historical context informs your interpretation. A performer who knows why a particular chord is significant plays it differently than one who doesn't.

**Experimenting in practice.** Trying different approaches (faster, slower, louder, softer, more aggressive, more gentle) helps you find what feels right. Not every experiment works, but the process reveals your preferences.

**Performing often.** Interpretation matures through performance. Each time you play a piece for an audience, you learn something about what works and what doesn't. Performers often say a piece takes years (or decades) to fully settle into.

**Engaging with the composer's world.** Reading biographies, listening to other works by the same composer, understanding the historical context, all these inform interpretation at a deeper level.

**Being yourself.** Ultimately, your interpretation reflects who you are. Your emotional range, your technical strengths, your aesthetic preferences, your life experiences all shape how you play. Faking a style that isn't yours tends to produce hollow performances.

## What makes an interpretation "good"

There's no objective answer. But several qualities tend to be recognized across traditions:

**Coherence.** A good interpretation has internal consistency. The choices make sense together, the tempo, dynamics, phrasing, and character form a unified vision.

**Musical judgment.** Phrases are shaped sensitively. The performer understands harmonic progressions and emphasizes what matters. Cadences land; climaxes peak; quiet moments breathe.

**Technical command.** The performer can execute their interpretive intentions. Nothing falls apart because of technical limitations. This doesn't mean flashy virtuosity, just control.

**Communication.** The performance reaches the listener. You feel something. You understand what the music is trying to say.

**Individuality.** There's a sense that *this* performer is playing this piece, not just any performer. The interpretation has personality.

Different traditions weight these qualities differently. Classical traditions might emphasize coherence and command. Jazz traditions might emphasize individuality and spontaneity. Folk traditions might emphasize communication and emotional connection. But these qualities are generally recognized across styles.

## The performer's responsibility

With interpretive freedom comes responsibility. A performer's choices affect:

**The composer.** Your interpretation represents the piece. A great performance can make a rarely-heard work famous; a weak one can turn audiences away from it.

**The audience.** The listener experiences the music through you. Your choices shape their experience.

**The tradition.** In classical music especially, you're participating in a long tradition of interpretation. Your performance joins the chain of performers who've played this piece before you.

**Yourself.** Performance is an act of personal expression. Every choice reflects who you are as a musician.

Good performers take this responsibility seriously. They work to serve the music rather than just display themselves. The best interpretations feel like the performer is in service to the piece, revealing its depth rather than imposing their personality onto it.

## Common questions

**How do I know if my interpretation is "right"?**

There's no single right answer. But good interpretations tend to satisfy multiple criteria: they honor the composer's markings, they work musically (phrases are coherent, cadences land, contrasts work), they feel expressive rather than mechanical, and they communicate to listeners. If multiple trusted musicians (teachers, fellow performers, audiences) respond positively, you're probably on a good track.

**Should I listen to recordings before performing a piece?**

Opinions differ. Some performers avoid recordings until they've developed their own interpretation, worrying that other performances will contaminate their ideas. Others listen extensively to learn the tradition. A middle path: develop your own initial approach first, then listen to several recordings, then refine your interpretation with the new input. Many teachers recommend this approach.

**Is it okay to deviate from markings in the score?**

Yes, sometimes, but deliberately, not by accident. Good performers know the markings and choose when to follow them strictly and when to deviate. Deviations should serve the music. Taking a slower tempo because you can't play fast enough is a technical limitation, not an interpretive choice. Taking a slower tempo because you think the music breathes better that way is a legitimate interpretive decision.

**Does interpretation matter in popular music?**

Absolutely. A pop song performed by different artists sounds different. Cover versions often reinterpret songs significantly. Jazz standards invite extensive reinterpretation. Even in music styles that might seem standardized (Broadway, country, pop), individual performers bring distinct interpretations. Interpretation is a universal skill across musical traditions.
