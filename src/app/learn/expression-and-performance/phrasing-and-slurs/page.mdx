## The intuition

A **slur** is a curved line that arches over several notes, indicating that those notes belong together as a single musical gesture. The slur tells the performer: these notes form a coherent unit, connect them smoothly, play them with a unified shape, treat them as a single expressive phrase.

Slurs serve two related but distinct purposes. A **phrasing slur** shows the large-scale shape of a musical sentence, the rise and fall of a phrase that might span 4, 8, or more measures. A **legato slur** is smaller and more technical, indicating that specific notes should be played smoothly connected (legato), with no articulation break between them.

Both types look similar on the page, and performers interpret context to know which is intended. Together, slurs tell the performer how to breathe through the music, how to group notes into gestures, sentences, and paragraphs.

## Phrasing slurs

A **phrasing slur** indicates the span of a musical phrase. It tells the performer where the phrase begins and ends, and (implicitly) where the phrase's shape peaks and falls.

Phrasing slurs are typically long (spanning 4, 8, or more measures) and align with the large-scale structure of the music. They're guidance for the shape of the phrase:

- Where the phrase rises (usually toward the peak)
- Where it falls (toward the cadence)
- How it breathes as a single unit

Performers interpret phrasing slurs by:

**Breath points.** The start and end of a phrasing slur suggest where to take an imaginary breath (or a real one, for singers and wind players). Within the slur, the player shouldn't stop or breathe; the notes are continuous as a unit.

**Dynamic shaping.** Phrasing slurs often align with dynamic shapes, subtle crescendos and decrescendos that shape the phrase from beginning to peak to end.

**Tempo shaping.** Phrases often have slight tempo variations within them, a subtle push through the middle, a slight settling at the end. These happen naturally within a well-phrased performance.

Phrasing slurs aren't about specific technical details; they're about the overall shape of the musical sentence. The performer reads them as structural guidance.

## Legato slurs

A **legato slur** is typically shorter and more specific, often covering 2-8 notes. It indicates that those specific notes should be played smoothly connected:

- **Wind players** play the slurred notes without re-tonguing; one breath carries through all of them.
- **String players** play the slurred notes on a single bow stroke (when possible).
- **Pianists** play the slurred notes with overlapping finger releases, so each note connects seamlessly to the next.
- **Singers** sing the slurred notes on a single syllable (often marked differently with a short curve underneath).

Legato slurs produce the "legato" articulation, smooth connection, no audible breaks between notes. They contrast with staccato (detached) or unmarked notes (which might be articulated either way depending on context).

## How to tell which kind of slur

Slurs can function as either phrasing or legato markings. How do you tell?

**Length.** Long slurs (many notes, several measures) are usually phrasing slurs. Short slurs (a few notes, within a single measure) are usually legato slurs.

**Overlap.** If slurs overlap or nest (a big slur with smaller slurs inside), the outer is phrasing, the inner are legato.

**Style context.** String players often see legato slurs (indicating bowing). Piano music sometimes blurs the distinction, a slur might mean both "play smoothly" and "this is one phrase."

**Performer's judgment.** Often the distinction doesn't matter much, a slur means "treat these notes as a unit," whether that unit is a small legato group or a large phrase. The performer interprets based on context.

## Ties vs. slurs, the same shape, different meaning

A **tie** and a **slur** both use curved lines. They're distinguished by context:

- **Tie:** connects two notes of the *same pitch*, holds them as one sustained sound.
- **Slur:** connects two or more notes of *different pitches*, plays them smoothly connected but each as a distinct note.

If you see a curve between two notes, look at whether those notes are the same pitch. Same pitch = tie. Different pitches = slur.

## Phrasing slurs in older vs. newer music

The use of phrasing slurs has evolved:

- **Baroque music** often has limited slur markings. Phrasing was largely a matter of performer convention, with specific styles (like French Baroque *notes inégales*) governing how notes were grouped.
- **Classical music** made phrasing slurs more explicit. Mozart's and Haydn's scores contain careful phrase markings, though sometimes sparser than modern standards.
- **Romantic music** often uses extensive phrasing slurs, with long slurs spanning entire phrases or even multiple phrases. Chopin's piano music is famous for its phrase markings that shape extended musical sentences.
- **Modern music** tends to be very explicit, if a composer wants a specific slur pattern, it's marked.

When performing older music, performers often add phrasing based on stylistic convention even when no slurs are marked. This is part of informed performance practice, reading between the lines of the score.

## Common questions

**Do I have to breathe at slur endings?**

Wind players and singers typically do, the slur marks natural breath points. String players and pianists don't breathe per se, but they produce a subtle pause or release at slur endings to indicate the end of the phrase. On piano, this often means a slight timing adjustment before the next phrase begins.

**What if a slur and a tie both appear on the same note?**

They usually don't, a tie connects two notes of the same pitch, so the second note is "absorbed" into the first. A slur between different pitches can't be the same line as a tie. If you see both markings, look carefully: you probably have a slur starting or ending at the same spot as a tie from elsewhere.

**Can a slur span multiple staves?**

Yes. Orchestral and keyboard scores often have slurs that span from one staff to another, a phrasing slur for a wind passage might arch from the flute staff to the clarinet staff, indicating that the whole phrase belongs to one voice even though it's distributed between instruments. This is especially common in orchestral reductions.

**Why are phrasing slurs sometimes ignored in modern editions?**

Partly because phrasing decisions were once seen as the performer's responsibility, not the composer's. Some older editions preserved only structural notation (notes and rhythms) and left phrasing to the player. Modern critical editions often add editorial phrasing suggestions, but performers still make final decisions based on their interpretation.
