## The intuition

Beyond the markings that shape individual notes (dynamics, articulation) and the markings that shape entire sections (tempo, phrasing), a final category of markings tells performers how to **organize themselves** or **physically play** their instruments. An orchestral string section might be told to split into multiple parts (*divisi*), to play a pizzicato, or to use mutes. A singer might be marked *solo* or *tutti*. These markings don't specify what to play as much as how to distribute it or how to produce the specific sound.

Texture markings are especially important in orchestral and ensemble scores. Without them, a score couldn't communicate whether the strings are all playing the same note or splitting into four-part harmony, whether a passage is soloistic or full ensemble, whether the brass are muted or not.

## Distribution markings

**Solo** — one player. Even if the marked part is written for a section (e.g., first violins), the solo marking tells only one player to perform that passage.

**Tutti** — all. After a solo, the tutti marking brings the entire section back to playing the part. Used especially in concertos (solo passages contrast with tutti orchestral passages).

**Divisi (div.)** — divided. A string section splits into multiple parts. If the score shows two notes on a single stem for the first violins, and "div." is marked, the violins are divided into two groups, each playing one of the notes. Without "div.", the section would play the double-stop (both notes together on each violin).

**Unisoni (unis.)** — unison. After a divisi section, unisoni brings the section back to a single part. Everyone plays the same notes together.

**A due (a 2)** — two. Usually applies to wind instruments like flutes or oboes. Two players on the same part play in unison. Can also be used in reverse — "first oboe only" or "second oboe only" for a single player.

**Sopra / Sotto** — above / below. Used occasionally for specific voice assignments.

## String-specific markings

**Pizzicato (pizz.)** — plucked. String players use their fingers to pluck the strings rather than using the bow. Produces a short, percussive sound.

**Arco** — with the bow. After a pizzicato passage, arco tells the players to use the bow again (returning to normal bowing technique).

**Col legno** — with the wood. The string player strikes the string with the wooden back of the bow rather than bowing with the hair. Produces a distinctive clacking sound.

**Sul ponticello (sul pont.)** — on the bridge. The player bows very close to the bridge, producing a thin, glassy, sometimes whistling sound.

**Sul tasto** — on the fingerboard. The player bows over the fingerboard rather than between the bridge and fingerboard. Produces a soft, flute-like tone.

**Spiccato** — a bouncing bow stroke, producing light staccato. More a technique than a marking, but sometimes written.

**Tremolo** — rapid repetition of a single note. Bowed instruments produce this with very fast back-and-forth bowing.

## Wind/brass-specific markings

**Con sordino (con sord.)** — with mute. Wind and brass players insert a mute into or onto their instrument, producing a muted, veiled sound.

**Senza sordino (senza sord.)** — without mute. Remove the mute and play normally.

**Flatterzunge (flutter-tongue)** — a technique where the wind/brass player trills their tongue (rolls an R sound) while playing, producing a rapid trilling sound.

**Glissando (gliss.)** — a slide from one note to another. On wind instruments, this might involve a sliding technique; on strings, it's a continuous slide.

**A due / a 2** — applies to wind instruments as described above.

**Stopped horn (+)** — on French horn, the player puts their hand into the bell to close it partially, producing a muffled, nasal sound.

## Keyboard-specific markings

**l.h. / r.h.** — left hand / right hand. Indicates which hand should play specific notes, especially when the assignment isn't obvious from the notation.

**Una corda** — on piano, indicates the use of the soft pedal (discussed on the Pedal Markings page).

**Tre corde** — remove the soft pedal, returning to full sound.

**Pedal markings (Ped., ✻, or bracket)** — use of the sustain pedal, as covered separately.

## Voice markings

**Soli** — multiple soloists (plural of solo).

**Tutti** — everyone, as described above.

**SATB, SATBB, SSA, etc.** — indicate the voice types (Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass) in the ensemble.

**A capella** — without instrumental accompaniment. The voices sing unaccompanied.

## Common questions

**What's the difference between div. and a due?**

Both mean splitting a section into parts, but *divisi* is mostly used for strings (where a section might split into 2, 3, or more parts), while *a due* is mostly used for winds (usually for two players on the same part, like first and second flute). *Divisi* is more flexible; *a due* is more specific.

**When should a string section play double-stops vs. divisi?**

When the score shows two notes on a single stem without a *divisi* marking, the section plays double-stops — each player plays both notes simultaneously. With *divisi*, the section splits. The default (no marking) is double-stops. Composers mark *divisi* explicitly when they want a split.

**Are texture markings always Italian?**

Mostly, following the convention for other performance terms. Some Italian terms have French or German equivalents in scores written in those languages (*avec sourdine* in French for "with mute," *mit Dämpfer* in German for the same). The Italian versions are the most universally understood.

**Do all orchestras follow these markings exactly?**

Generally, yes — the markings are part of the score and part of the composer's intention. Orchestras read them carefully. However, specific interpretive decisions (how to balance a divisi section, whether to use mutes of a particular kind) remain the conductor's and section leader's choice.
