## The intuition

Every beat in Western music can be split into smaller pieces. If you split a beat into two equal halves, you've got **simple meter**. If you split it into three equal thirds, you've got **compound meter**. Everything downstream — how the music feels, how you count it, how it dances — comes from which division is in use.

Most beginning students learn 3/4 and 6/8 and find them confusing because both have "three" in them somewhere. The confusion clears up instantly once you see that 3/4 has *three beats* in a measure (simple triple) and 6/8 has *two beats* that each divide into three (compound duple). Same number of eighth notes per measure; completely different feel.

## Simple meter

In **simple meter**, each beat divides naturally into **two equal parts**. The beat is a quarter note; the division is two eighth notes. The counting syllable pattern is "**1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and**" — each beat has one halfway point.

The most common simple meters:

- **Simple duple — 2/4** — two beats per measure, each divided in two
- **Simple triple — 3/4** — three beats per measure, each divided in two
- **Simple quadruple — 4/4** — four beats per measure, each divided in two

The "simple" refers to the *division of the beat*, not the complexity of the meter. A simple meter is one where the beat splits in half.

## Compound meter

In **compound meter**, each beat divides naturally into **three equal parts**. The beat is a dotted quarter note; the division is three eighth notes. The counting syllable pattern is "**1-and-a 2-and-a 3-and-a**" or "**1-2-3 4-5-6**" — each beat has three equal sub-parts.

The most common compound meters:

- **Compound duple — 6/8** — two beats per measure (each a dotted quarter), each divided into three
- **Compound triple — 9/8** — three beats per measure (each a dotted quarter), each divided into three
- **Compound quadruple — 12/8** — four beats per measure (each a dotted quarter), each divided into three

In compound meter, the bottom number of the time signature (usually 8) refers to the eighth note, but the eighth note is *not* the beat — it's the subdivision. The actual beat is the dotted quarter, equal to three eighth notes. This is the main thing to internalize about compound meter: the bottom number is a subdivision, not a beat.

## How to tell which you're in

Look at the top number of the time signature:

- **Top numbers 2, 3, 4** — simple meter. Beats are quarter notes (if bottom is 4), divided into halves.
- **Top numbers 6, 9, 12** — compound meter. Beats are dotted quarters (if bottom is 8), divided into thirds. Divide the top number by 3 to get the actual number of beats per measure (6/8 = 2 beats, 9/8 = 3 beats, 12/8 = 4 beats).

This shortcut works because compound time signatures are constructed by multiplying the number of beats by three (the subdivision count). A 6/8 measure has 2 × 3 = 6 eighth-note subdivisions. A 12/8 measure has 4 × 3 = 12.

The bottom number tells you what the *subdivision* is — usually 8, meaning eighth notes — but the *beat* is always a dotted version of that (dotted quarter = three eighths).

## Why compound meter exists

If compound meter is just triplets within a beat, why do we need a separate notation system? Why not write 6/8 music in 2/4 with triplet markings everywhere?

Two reasons.

**First, readability.** A piece that's consistently in threes — a gigue, a tarantella, a 6/8 lullaby — would be drowning in triplet markings if written in simple meter. Compound meter builds the triplet feel into the time signature itself, so the notation stays clean.

**Second, feel.** Compound meter isn't just "simple meter with triplets." The beat feels different — longer, more rolling, more dance-like. Music written natively in compound meter has a swing that simple-meter-plus-triplets doesn't fully capture.

A 6/8 jig feels rolling. A 2/4 march feels striding. Both have six eighth notes per measure; they're completely different experiences.

## Counting compound meter

A common beginner's mistake is counting every eighth note in 6/8, going "1-2-3-4-5-6." Technically correct, but it treats the eighth note as a beat, which it isn't. You end up hearing six separate pulses rather than two rolling beats.

The better approach: count the beats (the dotted quarters), feel the subdivisions inside them.

- 6/8: count "1-2-3, 2-2-3" (or "one-and-a, two-and-a") — two beats, each with three subdivisions
- 9/8: "1-2-3, 2-2-3, 3-2-3" — three beats
- 12/8: "1-2-3, 2-2-3, 3-2-3, 4-2-3" — four beats

Each "1" is a main beat. The "2" and "3" inside each group are subdivisions. Keeping the beat count low (2 in 6/8, 3 in 9/8, 4 in 12/8) helps you feel the meter naturally.

## Common questions

**Is 3/8 simple or compound?**

Simple. The top number 3 means three beats per measure, and each beat (an eighth note) divides into two sixteenths. 3/8 is simple triple, just written at a faster subdivision level than 3/4.

**What about 3/2?**

Also simple triple. Three half-note beats per measure, each dividing into two quarter notes. The pattern is the same as 3/4 — it's just notated with longer note values.

**Why do 6/4 and 3/2 look similar but feel different?**

6/4 is compound duple (two dotted-half beats, each dividing into three quarters). 3/2 is simple triple (three half-note beats, each dividing into two quarters). Same number of quarter notes per measure, different grouping. The time signature tells you which.

**Can I feel a 6/8 piece "in six" instead of "in two"?**

At a slow tempo, yes — and some slow 6/8 pieces are conducted and felt in six, with each eighth note getting its own beat-like emphasis. As tempo increases, the feel consolidates into two dotted-quarter beats. There's a gray zone at slower tempos where both interpretations work. At fast tempos, "in two" is almost always the felt reality.
