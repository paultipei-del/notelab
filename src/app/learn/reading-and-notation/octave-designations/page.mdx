## The intuition

Letter names tell you what pitch class a note belongs to (C, D, E, and so on) but they don't tell you *which* C, D, or E. A piano has eight Cs. An orchestra covers multiple octaves of every pitch. When a musician says "play a C," which C do they mean?

Octave designations fix this. By adding a number after the letter name, we specify exactly which octave the note sits in. **Middle C** becomes **C4**. The C one octave higher is **C5**. The C one octave lower is **C3**. This system (**scientific pitch notation**, or SPN) has become the standard way to refer unambiguously to specific pitches.

## How scientific pitch notation works

Every pitch gets a letter (A through G) and a number (0 through 8, typically) indicating its octave. The number changes when the letter cycles from B back to C. So counting upward from C4:

- C4, D4, E4, F4, G4, A4, B4
- Then: C5, D5, E5, F5, G5, A5, B5
- Then: C6, and so on

Notice that the number increments at C, not at A. This is a deliberate choice, it means a full octave (C to B) always shares the same number, which matches how musicians naturally think about octaves. The octave from C4 to B4 is "the fourth octave"; everything in it has a 4 after the letter.

Sharps and flats keep their octave number with them: **C♯4** is the C♯ in the fourth octave, **B♭3** is the B♭ in the third octave.

## The landmark Cs on a piano

An 88-key piano covers seven and a quarter octaves. The Cs on the keyboard are:

- **C1**, second-lowest C, about 32.7 Hz, deep bass range
- **C2**, pedal C, about 65.4 Hz, low bass
- **C3**, bass C, about 130.8 Hz, bottom of tenor range
- **C4**, **middle C**, about 261.6 Hz, the center of the keyboard
- **C5**, treble C, about 523.3 Hz, the C above middle C
- **C6**, "high C" for sopranos, about 1046.5 Hz
- **C7**, very high, about 2093 Hz
- **C8**, the highest C on a standard piano, about 4186 Hz

The lowest note on a standard 88-key piano is **A0** (about 27.5 Hz); the highest is **C8**.

## A4 = 440 Hz, the tuning reference

The pitch **A4**(the A above middle C) is the modern international tuning reference, set at **440 Hz**. Orchestras tune to this pitch; it's the note an oboist plays when the orchestra tunes at the start of a concert. Every other pitch in equal temperament is derived mathematically from A4's frequency.

Historical performance practice often uses slightly different references (A=415 Hz for Baroque, A=432 Hz for some tuning philosophies), but A4=440 Hz is the default worldwide for modern music.

## Why this system is useful

Scientific pitch notation is particularly valuable for:

- **Unambiguous communication.** "Play a C" is vague; "play C5" is precise.
- **Vocal range discussion.** A baritone's range might be roughly F2 to F4; a soprano's might be C4 to C6. The numbers communicate range instantly.
- **Software and MIDI.** Music software, digital instruments, and MIDI all use SPN or a close variant. Middle C is MIDI note 60, and knowing this lets you program or troubleshoot musical software.
- **Cross-instrument comparison.** Comparing the range of a flute (C4 to D7) with a cello (C2 to A5) is trivial in SPN, you can see at a glance where their ranges overlap.

## Alternative systems

A few other systems exist, mostly historical:

- **Helmholtz pitch notation** uses capital and lowercase letters with prime marks: middle C is written as *c′* (c-prime), the C above is *c′′*, and so on. Still used in some European and academic contexts, especially for historical musicology.
- **British pitch notation** is similar to Helmholtz, and older British texts sometimes number octaves differently from SPN.
- **Solfège with register indicators** (do¹, re², etc.) exists in some pedagogical traditions.

For practical purposes in the modern world, scientific pitch notation is the dominant system. It's what you'll see in software, in contemporary music theory texts, and in international musical communication.

## Common questions

**Is octave 0 always the lowest octave?**

Close, but it's not the absolute lowest. Octave 0 spans roughly 16 Hz to 32 Hz, most of which is below the threshold of audible pitch (about 20 Hz). A pipe organ's lowest pipes reach into octave 0, but most instruments and voices don't go that low.

**Why does the number change at C rather than A?**

The decision is somewhat arbitrary, but it was made to match how musicians think. A major scale starts on its tonic (C major starts on C), and grouping every pitch from C to B under a single octave number matches that mental organization. If the number changed at A, then "the fourth octave" would awkwardly split the C major scale across two numbers.

**How do I quickly find a specific note on the piano using SPN?**

Middle C is C4, at roughly the center of the keyboard. From there:
- The C to its right is C5 (one octave up)
- The C to its left is C3 (one octave down)
- All notes in the "4" octave are between C4 and the B just below C5

Finding any specific pitch is a matter of locating its letter name within the right octave band.

**What does it mean when a score lists a range like "C4–G5"?**

That the part goes as low as C4 (middle C) and as high as G5 (the G in the fifth octave). SPN makes ranges instantly readable. Compare to "middle C to the G above high C", same information, but much slower to parse.
