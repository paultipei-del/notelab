## The intuition

A triad has three notes: root, third, fifth. Add one more third on top (a seventh above the root) and you get a four-note chord. This **seventh chord** is richer than a triad, more directional (the seventh often wants to resolve), and carries more harmonic information than a plain triad can.

Seventh chords are everywhere in tonal music. The dominant seventh chord (V7) is arguably the single most important chord in Western tonal harmony, it drives most cadences, creates the tension that resolves to the tonic, and defines the sound of functional harmony. Beyond V7, other seventh-chord types add color in specific contexts and show up constantly in jazz, pop, and classical music from the Romantic period onward.

## The five common seventh-chord qualities

A triad is built from two 3rds (either major-minor for major triads, or minor-major for minor triads, etc.). A seventh chord adds one more 3rd on top. The five main seventh-chord qualities come from different combinations of triad quality and the specific seventh added on top.

**Major seventh (M7, △7, or Maj7)**
- Structure: major triad + major 7th
- From C: C-E-G-B
- Sound: bright, warm, sweet, slightly jazzy
- Most common context: built on I (I7) or IV (IV7) in major keys

**Dominant seventh (7)**
- Structure: major triad + minor 7th
- From C: C-E-G-B♭
- Sound: tense, wanting to resolve, bluesy
- Most common context: built on V (V7) in a major or minor key, the central tension chord of tonal harmony

**Minor seventh (m7)**
- Structure: minor triad + minor 7th
- From C: C-E♭-G-B♭
- Sound: dark, smooth, often jazzy
- Most common context: built on ii (ii7) or vi (vi7) in major keys, or i (i7) in minor

**Half-diminished seventh (ø7 or m7♭5)**
- Structure: diminished triad + minor 7th
- From C: C-E♭-G♭-B♭
- Sound: tense, dark, transitional
- Most common context: built on vii° in major (viiø7) or on ii° in minor (iiø7)

**Fully diminished seventh (°7 or dim7)**
- Structure: diminished triad + diminished 7th
- From C: C-E♭-G♭-B♭♭ (enharmonically C-E♭-G♭-A)
- Sound: very tense, highly chromatic, dramatic
- Most common context: built on vii° in harmonic minor (vii°7), a signature chord in minor-key drama

## Building seventh chords on any root

The pattern is consistent: take the triad quality you want, then add the appropriate 7th above the root. Some examples:

**G major 7:** G major triad (G-B-D) + major 7th above G (F♯) = G-B-D-F♯.

**A minor 7:** A minor triad (A-C-E) + minor 7th above A (G) = A-C-E-G.

**D dominant 7:** D major triad (D-F♯-A) + minor 7th above D (C) = D-F♯-A-C.

**F♯ half-diminished 7:** F♯ diminished triad (F♯-A-C) + minor 7th above F♯ (E) = F♯-A-C-E.

**B fully diminished 7:** B diminished triad (B-D-F) + diminished 7th above B (A♭) = B-D-F-A♭.

The quality of the triad and the quality of the added 7th are independent, any combination is theoretically possible, though only some are common in tonal music.

## Why seventh chords matter

Seventh chords do three things that triads can't do as well:

**1. They create directional pull.** A seventh chord, especially a dominant seventh, feels incomplete. The 7th of the chord "wants to resolve", typically downward by step. This directional pull is what makes V7 so powerful at cadences: the tritone between its 3rd and 7th resolves outward to the 3rd and root of the tonic chord.

**2. They add color without changing chord identity.** A C major triad is a stable chord. A C major 7 chord is still fundamentally C major but with more sonic depth, a sweeter, richer sound. Composers use seventh chords to add color to a progression without departing from the underlying harmonic structure.

**3. They enable richer harmonic vocabularies.** Jazz, in particular, is largely built on seventh-chord vocabulary. Nearly every chord in a jazz standard is a seventh chord of some kind. The specific qualities (M7, 7, m7, ø7) become the primary building blocks, with triads appearing less frequently than in classical music.

## Diatonic seventh chords in a major key

Just as you can build a triad on each scale degree, you can build a seventh chord on each scale degree by adding one more third.

In C major:

| Degree | Chord | Quality |
|--------|-------|---------|
| 1 (I7) | C-E-G-B | Major 7 |
| 2 (ii7) | D-F-A-C | Minor 7 |
| 3 (iii7) | E-G-B-D | Minor 7 |
| 4 (IV7) | F-A-C-E | Major 7 |
| 5 (V7) | G-B-D-F | **Dominant 7** |
| 6 (vi7) | A-C-E-G | Minor 7 |
| 7 (viiø7) | B-D-F-A | **Half-diminished 7** |

The pattern: M7, m7, m7, M7, **7**, m7, **ø7**. Every degree's seventh-chord quality is determined by the diatonic stacking of thirds in the scale.

Notice the **V7** (the only dominant 7 in the key) and the **viiø7**, the only half-diminished 7. These two chords are where the tritone interval appears (V7 has the tritone between its 3rd and 7th; viiø7 has it between its root and its 5th). They're the two tension chords of the key, the ones with the strongest pull toward resolution.

## Common questions

**What's the difference between a "dominant 7" chord (7) and a "major 7" chord (M7)?**

A **dominant 7** has a *minor* 7th above the root (producing the characteristic "bluesy" or "cadential" sound). A **major 7** has a *major* 7th above the root (producing a brighter, sweeter sound). Same major triad underneath; different 7th on top. They feel quite different, dominant 7 wants to resolve; major 7 is stable and can function as a tonic.

**Why is the dominant seventh specifically named that way?**

Because in its most important usage, it's built on the **dominant** (5th scale degree) of a key. V7 in C major is G-B-D-F, a dominant seventh chord whose quality is specifically called "dominant 7" because it's the chord that naturally appears on the dominant degree of a major scale. Even when the same chord structure (major triad + minor 7th) appears built on other scale degrees, it's still called a "dominant 7" quality by convention.

**What makes a "fully diminished" different from a "half diminished"?**

A **half-diminished 7** has a minor 7th above the root (e.g., C-E♭-G♭-B♭). A **fully diminished 7** has a diminished 7th above the root (e.g., C-E♭-G♭-B♭♭, which is enharmonically C-E♭-G♭-A). The "fully" version has every interval as minor-third-sized, making it a stack of four minor 3rds, unique among chord structures.

**Do I need to learn all five seventh-chord qualities?**

For fluent harmonic analysis and jazz, yes. For basic classical reading, the dominant 7 and maybe major 7 and minor 7 are most essential. Half-diminished and fully diminished are important for minor-key music and for specific functional contexts (leading-tone harmonies). Start with the dominant 7 (by far the most common), then expand outward.
