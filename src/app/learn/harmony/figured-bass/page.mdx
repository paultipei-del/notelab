## The intuition

In the Baroque era (roughly 1600–1750), composers wrote only the bass line and the melody, leaving the middle voices (the chords) to be filled in on the spot by a keyboard player or lutenist. To tell the performer which chords to play, composers added small numbers beneath the bass line. These numbers (**figured bass**) specified which intervals to include above the bass, enough information to determine the chord without writing out every note.

The practice of filling in chords from figured bass is now mostly historical (outside Baroque specialist performance), but the notation system lives on. Modern music theory uses figured bass symbols to indicate chord inversions, the exact same numbers, still attached to the same bass notes, still communicating the same information. Every time you see "I6" or "V6/5" or "vii°4/3" in a modern analysis, you're reading figured bass.

## The basic triad figures

For triads, there are three positions, each with its figured-bass signature:

**Root position: (nothing, or 5/3 for completeness).**

A root-position triad contains a 3rd and a 5th above its bass. Since these are the "default" intervals in a triad (a third above the root and a fifth above the root), they're not explicitly notated, the absence of figures means "root-position triad." Some texts write "5/3" to make this explicit, but in most modern analysis, root position has no figure at all.

**First inversion: 6 (or 6/3).**

A first-inversion triad has its third in the bass. The remaining chord tones form a 6th (to the root) and a 3rd (to the fifth, though now an inversion of the original fifth). Only the 6 is traditionally written, with the 3 assumed. So "6" after a Roman numeral indicates first inversion.

**Second inversion: 6/4.**

A second-inversion triad has its fifth in the bass. The other chord tones form a 6th (to the third) and a 4th (to the root). Both numbers are written because neither is assumed as default.

## Seventh chord figures

For seventh chords, there are four inversions, and each has a distinctive figure.

| Inversion | Bass note | Figure | Example (V7 in C major) |
|-----------|-----------|--------|-----|
| Root position | Root | 7 | V7 (G-B-D-F) |
| First inversion | 3rd | 6/5 | V6/5 (B-D-F-G) |
| Second inversion | 5th | 4/3 | V4/3 (D-F-G-B) |
| Third inversion | 7th | 2 (or 4/2) | V2 (F-G-B-D) |

The figures describe the intervals above the bass:

- **7**, in root position, a 7th above the bass is the seventh of the chord.
- **6/5**, in first inversion, the intervals above the bass include a 6th (to the original root) and a 5th (to the third).
- **4/3**, in second inversion, the intervals include a 4th (to the root) and a 3rd (to the fifth).
- **2** (often written as just "2"), in third inversion, the 2nd above the bass is the root; the full figure is 6/4/2, but it's abbreviated to just "2" because the 2nd alone is distinctive.

These four figures for seventh chords are essential to read in harmonic analysis. They come up constantly in real music, especially for the V7 chord and its resolution.

## Reading figured bass in context

In Baroque scores, you might see a bass line like:

> [bass note C, figure "6"]  [bass note D, figure "7"]  [bass note G, no figure]

Reading: the first bass note (C) takes a first-inversion chord (so chord built on A is implied, A-C-E). The second bass note (D) takes a seventh chord (D-F-A-C or similar). The third bass note (G) is root position, G major chord. The continuo player realizes this at the keyboard, improvising voicings and textures within the constraints the figures specify.

In modern analysis, the same information appears as:

> vi6 ... V7 ... I

- with figures attached to Roman numerals indicating chord identity and inversion simultaneously. The figures are the same (6, 7, etc.); just the surrounding labels differ.

## Accidentals in figured bass

Figured bass also uses accidentals to modify specific intervals. A natural, sharp, or flat before a figure indicates that the interval above the bass should be raised or lowered:

- **♯6**, the 6th above the bass is sharped
- **♭3**, the 3rd above the bass is flatted (or simply lowered)
- **5♭**, the 5th is flatted (with the flat to the right of the 5)

An accidental **without** a number refers to the 3rd by convention. So a lone sharp under a bass note means "raise the 3rd above the bass a half step", often used to produce a major quality where the key signature would otherwise produce minor.

These accidentals make figured bass extremely precise, you can specify any altered chord with a small handful of symbols.

## Why figured bass survived

Figured bass notation became obsolete as a performance practice by the late 18th century, classical composers began fully notating every pitch rather than leaving middle voices improvised. But the symbol system proved so clear and compact for indicating chord inversions that theorists kept using it for analysis.

Today, figured bass is:
- **The standard notation** for chord inversions in Roman numeral analysis
- **Essential knowledge** for students of Baroque music, who encounter original figured bass in performance scores
- **A useful shorthand** in composition sketches, where a composer might write a bass line with figures before filling in the full voicing

You don't need to be a Baroque keyboardist to use figured bass. But you do need to understand the symbols to read modern harmonic analysis fluently.

## Common questions

**Why are some figures abbreviated and others spelled out?**

Economy of notation. When a number is implicit (like the 3rd in first inversion, which is assumed), it's left out. When a combination uniquely identifies an inversion (like "6/4" for second inversion), both numbers are shown. The abbreviations evolved over centuries of Baroque practice, and they're now standardized. "6" always means first-inversion triad (with the 3 assumed). "6/4" always means second-inversion triad. "7" always means root-position seventh chord.

**What does "V7" mean exactly?**

V (Roman numeral) means the chord is built on the 5th scale degree. 7 (figured bass) means it's a seventh chord, so a seventh is added above the root of that 5th-degree triad. In C major, V7 = G-B-D-F. The combination of Roman numeral + figured bass completely specifies a chord's root, quality (by the Roman numeral's case), and inversion (by the figured-bass number).

**Do I have to learn all the seventh-chord inversion figures (6/5, 4/3, 2)?**

Yes, if you want to read harmonic analysis fluently. These come up constantly, especially for V7 and ii7 chords. Once you see them a few times, the pattern clicks: 7 → 6/5 → 4/3 → 2 goes with root → 1st → 2nd → 3rd inversion, and the numbers decrease in a consistent way.

**What's the difference between figured bass and a lead-sheet chord symbol?**

A lead-sheet symbol (like "Cm" or "G7") tells you the chord's identity (root and quality) but doesn't tell you which note is in the bass. A figured bass symbol tells you the inversion (bass note and chord structure) but assumes the chord quality comes from context (the key signature, the Roman numeral). They're complementary systems: lead sheet emphasizes chord identity; figured bass emphasizes inversion. In modern analysis, Roman numerals + figured bass gives you both.
