## The intuition

When you analyze a piece chord by chord, you'll notice that not every note in the melody belongs to the chord being played underneath it. A melody might linger on a note that clashes with the chord, slip down to a note between chord tones, or hit a dissonance before resolving to a consonance. These **non-chord tones** (sometimes called "non-harmonic tones") are what give melodies their life. Without them, melodies would just be arpeggiated chord tones, pretty, but often flat and predictable.

Non-chord tones come in several distinct types, each with specific rules about how it's approached and how it resolves. Learning to identify them is essential for analysis, and learning to use them is essential for composition. Most of what makes a melody feel "melodic" rather than "chordal" comes from the skillful placement of non-chord tones.

## The main types of non-chord tones

Non-chord tones are classified by *how they're approached* (from the previous note) and *how they're resolved* (to the next note).

**Passing tone (PT)**
- Approached by step
- Resolved by step in the same direction
- Fills in the melodic space between two chord tones
- Example: melody moves from C to E while a C chord is held, passing through D

Passing tones are the most common non-chord tones. They create smooth stepwise melodic lines between chord tones. In the example above, D is not in the C chord (which contains C, E, G), but it lives between the chord tones C and E, making the melodic line flow.

**Neighbor tone (NT)**
- Approached by step (up or down)
- Resolved by step back to the original note (opposite direction)
- Creates a momentary "detour" around a chord tone
- Example: melody holds on C, moves up to D briefly, then returns to C (while the C chord is sustained)

Neighbor tones decorate a chord tone without moving on. "Upper neighbor" if it goes up and comes back; "lower neighbor" if it goes down and comes back.

**Suspension (SUS)**
- A chord tone from the previous chord is held over into the new chord
- The held note is now a dissonance against the new chord
- It resolves downward by step to a chord tone of the new chord
- Example: in moving from a C chord (C-E-G) to a G chord (G-B-D), the E of the first chord is held; when the G chord arrives, E is dissonant (it's a 6th above G but not in the chord), then it resolves down by step to D

Suspensions are some of the most expressive non-chord tones. The held dissonance creates tension; the resolution down to a chord tone releases it. Baroque and Classical composers used suspensions constantly for expressive effect.

Suspensions are typically labeled by their intervals above the bass: a "4-3" suspension resolves from a 4th to a 3rd; a "7-6" resolves from a 7th to a 6th; a "9-8" resolves from a 9th to an octave.

**Anticipation (ANT)**
- Approached by step from a chord tone of the previous chord
- Stays in place (the note doesn't move) as the chord changes
- When the new chord arrives, the anticipating note is now a chord tone of the new chord
- Example: melody moves from E down to D while a C chord is held; the D is anticipating the G chord that arrives shortly, where D becomes a chord tone (the 5th of G)

Anticipations "arrive early" at the next chord's tone. They create a brief dissonance against the old chord that becomes consonant when the new chord appears.

**Appoggiatura (APP)**
- Approached by leap
- Resolved by step in the opposite direction of the leap
- A dissonant note emphasized by being approached via a leap
- Example: over a C chord, the melody leaps up to F, then steps down to E

Appoggiaturas create a strong accent on a dissonance. The leap into the note draws attention; the subsequent stepwise resolution releases the tension. Appoggiaturas are especially common in Classical and Romantic music as expressive "sighs" or "leanings."

**Escape tone (ET)**, sometimes called *échappée*
- Approached by step
- Resolved by leap in the opposite direction
- A kind of "escape" from the melodic line's direction
- Example: melody moves C-D-B (the D is approached by step from C, then leaps down to B, which is a chord tone)

Escape tones are less common but appear in specific ornamented styles. They provide a brief decorative turn in the melodic line.

**Pedal point (PED)**
- A sustained note (usually in the bass) that persists through changing chords above it
- Against the shifting harmonies, the pedal is sometimes a chord tone and sometimes a non-chord tone
- Example: a long-held tonic note in the bass while the upper voices move through various chords

Pedal points are especially common in climaxes, transitions, and endings. The tension of chords changing above a held bass note builds momentum until the pedal finally joins the texture as a chord tone.

**Accented vs. unaccented non-chord tones**

Non-chord tones can be **accented** (falling on a strong beat, creating emphasized dissonance) or **unaccented** (falling on a weak beat, passing by more subtly). Accented non-chord tones have more expressive weight; unaccented ones smooth melodic lines without drawing attention.

A suspension is always accented, the held note falls on a strong beat of the new chord. A passing tone is typically unaccented, it passes between chord tones without emphasis.

## Why non-chord tones matter

Non-chord tones do several things:

**They create melodic motion.** Without non-chord tones, a melody would only contain chord tones, an arpeggio. Melodies sound like melodies because they weave chord tones and non-chord tones together into fluid lines.

**They smooth voice leading.** Stepwise connections between chords are usually smoother than leaps. Passing tones fill gaps; neighbor tones add graceful turns. Voice leading becomes less angular with non-chord tones.

**They create expressive tension.** Suspensions and appoggiaturas produce momentary dissonance that resolves to consonance, the microcosm of tonal music's tension-release dynamic. Many of the most expressive moments in classical music are created by non-chord tones.

**They allow richer harmony.** Non-chord tones let composers use simpler underlying chord progressions (maybe just I, IV, V) while producing melodic complexity. The chord tells you the harmony; the non-chord tones tell you where the emotional action is.

## Identifying non-chord tones in analysis

When you analyze a piece, you identify non-chord tones by:

1. **Determining the underlying chord** at each beat (or at each harmonic unit).
2. **Checking each melodic note** against the chord. If the note is in the chord, it's a chord tone. If not, it's a non-chord tone.
3. **Classifying the non-chord tone** by its approach and resolution (passing, neighbor, suspension, etc.).

This process reveals the harmonic structure of a piece separately from its melodic surface. Two pieces might have identical chord progressions but feel very different because of their different non-chord tone usage.

## Common questions

**How do I tell a passing tone from a neighbor tone?**

A passing tone continues in the same direction after stepping to the non-chord tone (up, up, up, or down, down, down). A neighbor tone reverses direction (up, down, or down, up). Both are approached and resolved by step; the difference is whether the motion continues or turns back.

**What's the difference between a suspension and an appoggiatura?**

A **suspension** is held over from the previous chord (it was a chord tone before; now it's dissonant because the chord changed). An **appoggiatura** is approached by a leap into the dissonance (it wasn't part of the previous chord at all). Both are accented dissonances that resolve by step, but their approach is different, suspensions are "passive" (the note was already there); appoggiaturas are "active" (the voice leaps into the dissonance).

**Are non-chord tones used in jazz and pop?**

Extensively. Jazz melodies are full of non-chord tones, often extended chord tones (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) that aren't in the basic chord. Pop vocals constantly use passing tones, neighbor tones, and appoggiaturas to add expressive color. The specific vocabulary varies by style, but non-chord tones are universal to melodic writing.

**Do I need to identify all non-chord tones to understand a piece?**

For deep analysis, yes, they reveal how melodies are constructed and what makes them expressive. For casual listening, no, you can enjoy the music without classifying every note. But once you learn to identify them, you hear melodies differently. You start noticing the specific moments of dissonance and resolution that create the music's emotional texture.
