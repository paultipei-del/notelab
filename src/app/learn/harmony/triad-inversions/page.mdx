## The intuition

A triad (C-E-G) is the same chord regardless of which note sits in the bass. But the sound changes depending on which chord tone is on the bottom. With C in the bass, it's called root position — the default, most stable voicing. With E in the bass, it's first inversion. With G in the bass, it's second inversion.

These three inversions are just three different ways of arranging the same triad. They share the same chord identity but differ in voicing, stability, and typical usage. Understanding them is essential because real music uses all three — and the specific inversion often signals something about the chord's function.

## Root position

**Bass note: the root.**

A triad in **root position** has its root in the bass. For a C major triad, C is in the bass. For an E minor triad, E is in the bass.

Root position is the most stable form of a triad. The chord's identity matches its lowest sounding note — the chord literally stands on its root. This is the default position for stable, arrival-point chords (the tonic at the end of a phrase, most resting points in a progression).

When analyzing harmony, root-position triads are labeled with just the Roman numeral (e.g., "I" or "vi" or "V"). No figured-bass numbers are added.

## First inversion

**Bass note: the third.**

A triad in **first inversion** has its third in the bass. For a C major triad, E is in the bass, with C and G somewhere above. The chord is still a C major triad — same three pitches — but the lowest note is now the third, not the root.

**Figured bass:** a **6** is added after the Roman numeral (e.g., "I6"). This indicates that a sixth (counted from the bass note) is present in the chord — specifically, the interval from E (bass) to C (root of the original triad) is a minor 6th. The 6 is shorthand for "the chord has a 6 above the bass," which is the signature of first inversion.

Technically the figure should be "6/3" (indicating both the 6th and 3rd above the bass), but "6" alone is the standard abbreviation — the 3rd is assumed.

**Sound:** slightly less stable than root position, but still fairly solid. First-inversion triads are common in mid-progression voicings, in bass lines that move stepwise (rather than by leap), and in passages where the root note would be awkward to have in the bass.

**Example:** C/E (written with a slash in pop-chord notation) or I6 (in Roman numeral analysis) — both mean "C major triad with E in the bass."

## Second inversion

**Bass note: the fifth.**

A triad in **second inversion** has its fifth in the bass. For a C major triad, G is in the bass, with C and E somewhere above.

**Figured bass:** a **6/4** is added after the Roman numeral (e.g., "I6/4"). This indicates that both a 6th and a 4th above the bass are present — from G (bass) to E is a major 6th, and from G to C is a perfect 4th.

**Sound:** the least stable of the three inversions. Having the fifth in the bass creates an unusual harmonic tension — the chord feels like it's "leaning" on its fifth rather than standing on its root or third. Second-inversion triads don't usually appear as long-held chords because the instability is too obvious. Instead, they serve specific functional purposes.

**Common uses for 6/4 chords:**

1. **Cadential 6/4** — appears at a cadence, functioning as an embellishment of the dominant (V). Usually written "I6/4" but analyzed as V with a delayed resolution. The quintessential strong cadence is "I6/4 → V → I" — the tonic 6/4 resolves through the V to the tonic.

2. **Passing 6/4** — appears in stepwise bass motion, where the bass is moving from one note to another and the chord passes through a 6/4 position.

3. **Pedal 6/4** (or neighboring 6/4) — appears over a held bass note, with the upper voices briefly moving through a 6/4 figure before returning.

In all three uses, the 6/4 is always treated as a special, unstable chord that needs specific handling. You wouldn't end a piece on a 6/4 chord — it would feel unfinished.

## Figured bass notation

The three positions of a triad use these figured-bass symbols:

| Inversion | Bass note | Figured bass | Roman numeral example |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| Root position | Root | (none) | I |
| First inversion | Third | 6 | I6 |
| Second inversion | Fifth | 6/4 | I6/4 |

These figures are added after the Roman numeral to specify which inversion of the chord is being used. They're short, unambiguous, and universal in harmonic analysis.

## Why inversions matter

Inversions aren't just abstract theory — they're deeply practical:

**Smoother bass lines.** A bass line that moves smoothly (by step) is more singable and more graceful than one that jumps around. Inversions let the bass stay smooth while the chord progression proceeds. If a progression goes C → F, the bass would have to leap from C to F in root position. But if the F is in first inversion (F/A), the bass moves from C up to A — smoother. Inversions give composers flexibility in bass-line writing.

**Specific functional effects.** The cadential 6/4 has a distinctive sound that only works in that specific context. First-inversion chords tend to sound more "moving" or "transitional" than root-position chords, which tend to sound more "stable" or "arrived."

**Voicing variety.** Even without specific function, inversions give composers choices about how to voice each chord. The same progression can feel dramatically different depending on which inversions are chosen at each step.

**Reading skill.** Real music uses inversions constantly. A pianist who can only recognize root-position chords will struggle with most repertoire. Reading fluency includes identifying inversions quickly.

## Common questions

**Why is the figured bass "6" for first inversion and "6/4" for second inversion?**

Because these figures describe the intervals from the bass note up to the chord's other tones. In first inversion (third in bass), the root is a 6th above the bass (so "6" is the identifying interval). In second inversion (fifth in bass), the root is a 4th above the bass and the third is a 6th above, so both "6" and "4" are specified. The numbers describe what's literally present in the chord relative to the bass.

**Do inversions change the chord's quality?**

No. A C major triad is C major in every inversion — major quality is determined by the interval structure of the chord's three notes, not by which is in the bass. The quality is invariant; only the voicing changes.

**How do I identify which inversion a chord is in?**

Look at the bass note. If it matches the chord's root, it's root position. If it's the chord's third, first inversion. If it's the chord's fifth, second inversion. (For seventh chords, there's also a third inversion where the seventh is in the bass.) Once you identify the chord's root and which chord tone is in the bass, the inversion follows automatically.

**Why are 6/4 chords treated as unstable when the notes are the same as a stable chord in root position?**

Because hearing matters more than theory. A chord with its 5th in the bass feels unstable to the ear, even though the three pitch classes are identical to a stable chord with the root in the bass. The ear assigns "stability" partly based on which note is lowest, and the 5th-in-the-bass position creates a characteristic open, "waiting" sound that doesn't resolve.
