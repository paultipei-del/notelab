## The intuition

A scale isn't just a set of notes to melody with, it's also a supply depot for chords. Stack thirds on any note of the scale (using only notes from the scale), and you get a triad. Seven scale degrees, seven triads. These seven triads form the **diatonic chord vocabulary** of a key, the default chords available in that key without reaching for outside pitches.

Every piece of tonal music draws from this vocabulary. Most of the chords you hear in major or minor-key music are one of seven triads built on the seven degrees of that key's scale. Learning this pattern for major and minor scales gives you instant access to the chord set of any key.

## Diatonic triads of the major scale

Build a triad on each scale degree, stacking thirds within the scale:

In C major (pitches C, D, E, F, G, A, B):

| Degree | Root | Third | Fifth | Chord | Quality |
|--------|------|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | C | E | G | C | **Major** |
| 2 | D | F | A | Dm | **Minor** |
| 3 | E | G | B | Em | **Minor** |
| 4 | F | A | C | F | **Major** |
| 5 | G | B | D | G | **Major** |
| 6 | A | C | E | Am | **Minor** |
| 7 | B | D | F | B° | **Diminished** |

The pattern of qualities in major:

**Major, minor, minor, Major, Major, minor, diminished**

This pattern is invariant across every major key. G major has G-Am-Bm-C-D-Em-F♯°, with the same quality sequence. F major has F-Gm-Am-B♭-C-Dm-E°. Every major key shares this exact pattern of chord qualities on each scale degree.

## Diatonic triads of the natural minor scale

In A minor (pitches A, B, C, D, E, F, G):

| Degree | Root | Third | Fifth | Chord | Quality |
|--------|------|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | A | C | E | Am | **Minor** |
| 2 | B | D | F | B° | **Diminished** |
| 3 | C | E | G | C | **Major** |
| 4 | D | F | A | Dm | **Minor** |
| 5 | E | G | B | Em | **Minor** |
| 6 | F | A | C | F | **Major** |
| 7 | G | B | D | G | **Major** |

The pattern of qualities in natural minor:

**minor, diminished, Major, minor, minor, Major, Major**

This pattern is also invariant across every minor key. E natural minor has Em-F♯°-G-Am-Bm-C-D. D natural minor has Dm-E°-F-Gm-Am-B♭-C. The quality sequence stays constant.

Notice that natural minor's quality pattern is the same as the major pattern, just rotated to start on the 6th position of major. This follows from A minor being the relative minor of C major, same pitches, different starting point, and therefore the triads line up at different scale positions.

## Harmonic minor's altered chords

When you apply harmonic minor's raised 7th to building triads, some chords change:

In A harmonic minor (A, B, C, D, E, F, G♯):

| Degree | Root | Third | Fifth | Chord | Quality |
|--------|------|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | A | C | E | Am | Minor |
| 2 | B | D | F | B° | Diminished |
| 3 | C | E | **G♯** | C+ | **Augmented** |
| 4 | D | F | A | Dm | Minor |
| 5 | E | **G♯** | B | E | **Major** |
| 6 | F | A | C | F | Major |
| 7 | **G♯** | B | D | G♯° | **Diminished** |

The raised 7th (G♯) changes three chords:
- **III** becomes augmented (C+)
- **V** becomes major (major E, with the leading tone G♯)
- **vii°** is now a diminished triad built on the raised 7th

The crucial one is **V**, turning minor into major is exactly what makes the V-i cadence work in minor keys. This is why harmonic minor exists.

Composers in minor keys blend natural minor (for much of the melodic material) with harmonic minor (for dominant-tonic resolutions). The chord vocabulary of a minor key is a hybrid, drawing from both forms depending on what the music needs at each moment.

## Why this matters

Knowing the diatonic triads of a key gives you enormous analytical power:

- **You can identify chords quickly.** Seeing a C major chord in a piece in C major, you know it's I (the tonic). Seeing Dm in C major, you know it's ii.
- **You can predict chord progressions.** Certain chord movements (V to I, ii to V, I to IV) are extremely common. Knowing which chord is which in the key reveals these patterns.
- **You can transpose easily.** The pattern of qualities is the same in every major key. Learning "I-IV-V-I" in C means you know it in every key, just apply the same qualities to each new key's scale degrees.
- **You can compose within a key.** If you want to write in C major, the seven diatonic triads are your default palette. Chromatic chords (from outside the key) provide variety, but the diatonic triads are home.

## The expected locations of each quality

In both major and minor keys, each quality has its "natural home":

**In major keys:**
- Major triads on scale degrees 1, 4, 5 (I, IV, V, the primary chords)
- Minor triads on scale degrees 2, 3, 6 (ii, iii, vi, the secondary chords)
- Diminished triad on scale degree 7 (vii°)

**In natural minor keys:**
- Minor triads on scale degrees 1, 4, 5 (i, iv, v, the primary chords)
- Major triads on scale degrees 3, 6, 7 (III, VI, VII, the secondary chords)
- Diminished triad on scale degree 2 (ii°)

**In harmonic minor:**
- V becomes major (because of the raised 7th)
- vii° becomes diminished (built on the raised 7th)
- III becomes augmented (with the raised 7th as its 5th)

Understanding these locations lets you instantly recognize chord function when you identify which degree a triad is built on.

## Common questions

**Why does the major scale produce only one diminished triad?**

Because the diminished triad requires both a minor 3rd above the root *and* a diminished 5th above the root (which is the tritone). The only scale degree in a major scale where both these intervals are produced from within the scale is the 7th degree. Hence, only one diminished triad, on scale degree 7.

**How do I remember the pattern of qualities in major?**

The common mnemonic is "Major-minor-minor-Major-Major-minor-diminished", the pattern goes I-ii-iii-IV-V-vi-vii°. Some people memorize it as capital and lowercase letters (IMMmmM°, uppercase for major qualities, lowercase for minor, with a symbol for diminished). Once you build a dozen or so diatonic triads by ear, the pattern becomes automatic.

**What about the augmented triad? Do any scale degrees produce one?**

In natural minor and major, no, neither scale produces an augmented triad from its own notes. The augmented triad appears in harmonic minor on the 3rd scale degree (because of the raised 7th acting as the 5th of that chord). It also appears when composers use chromatic alterations beyond a single scale. Augmented triads are relatively rare in diatonic music.

**Can you transpose a diatonic triad to another key easily?**

Yes, and that's one of the huge advantages of knowing the pattern of qualities. The ii chord is always minor, whether in C major (Dm), G major (Am), or B♭ major (Cm). The V chord is always major (except in natural minor). Once you know the pattern, you can build any chord in any key without starting from scratch.
