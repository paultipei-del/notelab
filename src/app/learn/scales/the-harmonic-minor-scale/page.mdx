## The intuition

Natural minor has a problem: no proper leading tone. The 7th degree is a whole step below the tonic, which produces a weak pull toward resolution. In tonal music, where the dominant chord's pull back to the tonic is foundational, this weakness matters.

The solution is simple. Raise the 7th degree by a half step. That one change makes the 7th a half step below the tonic (now a leading tone) and restores the strong V-i harmonic resolution. The resulting scale is the **harmonic minor scale** — essentially natural minor with a raised 7th, used specifically for harmonic purposes.

The name tells you what it's for. It's the minor scale adjusted to make proper tonal harmony work.

## The interval pattern

Harmonic minor's pattern, with the raised 7th:

**Whole - Half - Whole - Whole - Half - Augmented 2nd - Half**

Or by scale degrees:
- 1st to 2nd: whole step
- 2nd to 3rd: half step
- 3rd to 4th: whole step
- 4th to 5th: whole step
- 5th to 6th: half step
- 6th to 7th: **augmented 2nd (three half steps)** — the distinctive new interval
- 7th to 8th: half step

The big change from natural minor is the 6th-to-7th interval. In natural minor, that's a whole step. In harmonic minor, it's an augmented 2nd — a half step larger than a whole step, because the 7th has been raised while the 6th stayed low.

## Building any harmonic minor scale

Start from the natural minor form, then raise the 7th:

**A harmonic minor starting from A:**
- Natural minor: A, B, C, D, E, F, G, A
- Raise the 7th: G becomes G♯
- A harmonic minor: A, B, C, D, E, F, **G♯**, A

**D harmonic minor:**
- Natural minor: D, E, F, G, A, B♭, C, D
- Raise the 7th: C becomes C♯
- D harmonic minor: D, E, F, G, A, B♭, **C♯**, D

The raised 7th is accidentally notated in the music (not written into the key signature, which still reflects the natural minor). When you see a piece in a minor key, you often see the raised 7th appearing as an accidental — that's the harmonic minor showing up within the natural-minor key signature.

## The augmented 2nd — harmonic minor's distinctive sound

The augmented 2nd between the 6th and 7th degrees is harmonic minor's most recognizable feature. It's a larger melodic jump than a whole step but smaller than a minor 3rd — an interval that sounds exotic, almost Middle Eastern or Hungarian in flavor.

In some musical traditions (Klezmer, Romani, Arabic-influenced music), the augmented 2nd is a desired coloring and appears prominently. In Classical and Romantic minor-key music, though, composers often avoid landing on the augmented 2nd melodically — it sounds awkward in purely stepwise melodic motion, like a miniature leap that breaks the flow.

This is why melodic minor exists. It's a modified version of harmonic minor that sidesteps the augmented 2nd for melodic contexts. We'll cover it next.

## When to use harmonic minor

Harmonic minor gets used when you need a leading tone — which is essentially whenever you're constructing or analyzing a V-i cadence in a minor key. The V chord of a minor key needs the raised 7th to be a major chord with a leading tone, which produces the strong pull to the tonic.

Example: in A minor, the V chord is E. In natural minor, E would be E-G-B (because the 7th of the scale, G, is natural). That's an E minor chord. E minor moving to A minor doesn't have the strong leading-tone pull of major-V-to-minor-i.

With the raised 7th from harmonic minor, the V chord becomes E-G♯-B — an E major chord. The G♯ is now a leading tone, pulling strongly up to A. E major to A minor has the classic V-i cadence feel.

So harmonic minor's primary function is *harmonic*: it provides the raised 7th needed for a proper V chord in a minor key. Composers use it when building chord progressions, and singers/instrumentalists learn it because its melodic shape appears constantly in minor-key music.

## Common questions

**Why not just always use harmonic minor and forget natural minor?**

Because melody sounds different from harmony. The augmented 2nd between the 6th and 7th degrees is harmonically useful but melodically awkward. For melodic lines, composers often prefer natural minor's simpler stepwise motion or melodic minor's adjusted shape. Harmonic minor is best thought of as "the minor scale's harmonic form" — useful when you need the leading tone for chord progressions, less ideal when you need smooth melodic motion through all seven degrees.

**Is the raised 7th always written with an accidental, or does it appear in the key signature?**

Always with an accidental. The key signature for any minor key reflects the natural minor form. The raised 7th is a "temporary alteration" that appears as an accidental within the music. This is why minor-key pieces are full of sharps that aren't in the key signature — those are the raised 7ths of harmonic minor.

**Are there scales with other raised degrees besides harmonic minor?**

Yes, many. Melodic minor raises both the 6th and 7th (ascending only). Some Eastern-European and Middle Eastern scales raise different degrees. There's even a "double harmonic" scale that raises two degrees with an augmented 2nd in two places. Harmonic minor is the most common and the most deeply integrated into Western tonal music, but it's one of many possible scale modifications.

**Does harmonic minor work the same on descending motion?**

Yes. The raised 7th stays raised whether you're ascending or descending in harmonic minor. The scale is identical in both directions. This is different from melodic minor, where the scale changes shape depending on direction.
